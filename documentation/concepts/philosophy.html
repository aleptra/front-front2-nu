Philosophy

Front embraces a declarative, low-code philosophy that simplifies development by unifying structure, behavior, and styling into a clear, coherent workflow.

Origin Story

The spark for Front and FTML was ignited in 2019 by Josef Gabrielsson. Existing frameworks had become fragmented, requiring a dizzying array of files even for simple tasks. This exploration evolved into a low-code vision: reduce the code developers must orchestrate manually and express intent directly through structured markup.

At the time, the industry was deep into the "separation of concerns" dogma. Proposing a way to program directly within markup was often met with skepticism.

The Challenge

Creating a new paradigm meant overcoming friction and the "mental tax" of context switching and technical complexity, refined over years of experimentation before public release.

The Solution

Front and FTML form a declarative, unified framework where markup is the authoritative source of truth, weaving structure, behavior, and styling into a single coherent thread.

  • Simplicity: Reduce boilerplate and unnecessary files.
  • Clarity: Intent is visible directly in markup.
  • Productivity: Teams focus on delivering value rather than wrestling with technical overhead.

A Different Mental Model

Front shifts how interfaces are conceived: instead of assembling behavior step by step, developers describe what elements are and how they behave, letting the framework interpret intent.

Reducing Cognitive Load

By keeping structure, behavior, and layout together, Front minimizes context switching and fragmented abstractions, letting developers reason about interfaces more intuitively.

Pragmatism Over Purity

Front favors practical cohesion over strict separation between technologies, organizing concerns around clarity and outcomes rather than file type.

Tooling Should Fade Into the Background

The framework aims to feel almost invisible, removing ceremony and heavy setup so that focus remains on the application itself.

The Guiding Idea

Developing for the web should feel closer to composing a document than engineering a machine. By collapsing unnecessary layers and emphasizing declarative expression, Front restores a direct path between intention and implementation.
